Chunyang Wang: Local Gibbs Sampling beyond Local Uniformity

Friday, 2025-11-21, 12:00

Local samplers are algorithms that generate random samples based on local queries to high-dimensional distributions, ensuring the samples follow the correct induced distributions while maintaining time complexity that scales locally with the query size. These samplers have broad applications, including deterministic approximate counting~[He, Wang, Yin, SODA '23; Feng et.al., FOCS '23], sampling from infinite or high-dimensional Gibbs distributions~[Anand, Jerrum, SICOMP '22; He, Wang, Yin, FOCS '22], and providing local access to large random objects~[Biswas, Rubinfield, Yodpinyanee, ITCS '20].In this work, we present local samplers for Gibbs distributions of spin systems. Specifically, we design linear-time local samplers for spin systems with soft constraints, including the first local sampler for near-critical Ising models, as well as for truly repulsive spin systems, represented by the first local sampler for uniform proper $q$-colorings, with $q=O(\Delta)$ colors on graphs with maximum degree $\Delta$.
These local samplers are efficient beyond the ``local uniformity'' threshold, which imposes unconditional marginal lower bounds --- a key assumption required by all prior local samplers. Our results show that, in general, local sampling is not significantly harder than global sampling for spin systems. As an application, our results also imply local algorithms for probabilistic inference in the same near-critical regimes.

Halley Goldberg: Probabilistic Kolmogorov Complexity with Applications to Average-Case Complexity

Friday, 2025-11-14, 12:00

It is a central open problem in complexity theory to understand the relationship between worst-case and average-case complexity, particularly of the class NP. Hirahara (STOC 2021) made exciting progress toward this goal by applying techniques from meta-complexity. He showed, for example, that if DistNP^NP ⊆ AvgP then NP ⊆ TIME[2^{O(n/log n)}]. However, the original proof does not survive the setting of probabilistic computation.
In this talk, I will discuss the introduction of a probabilistic variant of time-bounded Kolmogorov complexity, called pK^t complexity, which allows the above limitation to be overcome. In the few years since the aforementioned work, pK^t complexity has also found broader utility for meta-complexity and related subjects.
Starting from basic definitions and background, the talk will lead to a proof overview of the following statement: if DistNP^NP ⊆ AvgBPP then NP ⊆ BPTIME[2^{O(n/log n)}].
Based on joint work with Valentine Kabanets, Zhenjian Lu, and Igor Oliveira.

Petr Chmel: String graphs with precise number of intersections

Friday, 2025-11-07, 12:00

A string graph is an intersection graph of curves in the plane. A k-string graph is a graph with a string representation in which every pair of curves intersects in at most k points. In this talk, we introduce the class of (=k)-string graphs as a further restriction of k-string graphs by requiring that every two curves intersect in either zero or precisely k points. We study the hierarchy of these graphs, showing that for any k ≥ 1, (=k)-string graphs are a subclass of (=k+2)-string graphs as well as of (=4k)-string graphs; however, there are no other inclusions between the classes of (=k)-string and (=ℓ)-string graphs apart from those that are implied by the above rules. In particular, the classes of (=k)-string graphs and (=k+1)-string graphs are incomparable by inclusion for any k, and the class of (=2)-string graphs is not contained in the class of (=2ℓ+1)-string graphs for any ℓ.
Joint work with Vít Jelínek.

Lucas Picasarri-Arrieta (NII): Chromatic discrepancy of locally s-colourable graphs

Friday, 2025-10-31, 12:00

The chromatic discrepancy of a graph G, denoted φ(G), is the least over all proper colourings σ of G of the greatest difference between the number of colours |σ(V (H))| spanned by an induced subgraph H of G and its chromatic number χ(H). It follows easily from the definition that every graph G satisfies φ(G) ∈ [0, χ(G)-1]. In this talk, we will see that locally colourable graphs actually have chromatic discrepancy close to the trivial upper bound χ(G)-1. In particular, we will show that triangle free graphs have chromatic discrepancy either χ(G)-2 or χ(G)-1, hence confirming the remaining open case of a conjecture of Aravind, Kalyanasundaram, Sandeep, and Sivadasan. Along the way, we will prove an (almost) tight bound on the chromatic number of balls of radius ℓ/2 in graphs excluding the cycle on ℓ+1, which could be of independent interest.
Joint work with Timothée Corsini, Théo Pierron, François Pirot, and Eileen Robinson.
Preprint available on arxiv.

Michal Koucky: Collapsing catalytic classes

Friday, 2025-08-20, 12:00

A catalytic machine is a space-bounded Turing machine with additional access to a second, much larger work tape, with the caveat that this tape is full, and its contents must be preserved by the computation. Catalytic machines were defined by Buhrman et al. (STOC 2014), who, alongside many follow-up works, exhibited the power of catalytic space (CSPACE) and in particular catalytic logspace machines (CL) beyond that of traditional space-bounded machines.
Several variants of CL have been proposed, including non-deterministic and co-non-deterministic catalytic computation by Buhrman et al. (STACS 2016) and randomized catalytic computation by Datta et al. (CSR 2020). These and other works proposed several questions, such as catalytic analogues of the theorems of Savitch and Immerman and Szelepcsényi. Catalytic computation was recently derandomized by Cook et al. (STOC 2025), but only in certain parameter regimes.
We settle almost all questions regarding randomized and non-deterministic catalytic computation, by giving an optimal reduction from catalytic space with additional resources to the corresponding non-catalytic space classes. With regards to non-determinism, our main result is that CL=CNL and with regards to randomness we show CL=CPrL where CPrL denotes randomized catalytic logspace where the accepting probability can be arbitrarily close to 1/2. In this talk I will explain the model, the results and give overview of the techniques.
Joint work with Ian Mertz, Edward Pyne and Sasha Sami

Sarah Houdaigoui (NII): A quasi-polynomial bound for the minimal excluded minors for a surface

Friday, 2025-08-22, 12:00

Using the Graph Minor Theorem, by Robertson and Seymour, one can easily prove that there are only a finite number of excluded minors for a given surface. This immediately implies that, given a surface, the size of these excluded minors is bounded. However, no explicit bound can be derived from the Graph Minor Theorem. In 1993, Seymour proved that an excluded minor for a given surface S is of order bounded by a double exponential of the genus of S. More than thirty years later, we managed to lower this bound to a quasi-polynomial in the genus of the surface.
Joint work with Ken-ichi Kawarabayashi.

Marcelo Garlet Milani (NII): Directed disjoint paths remains W[1]-hard on acyclic digraphs without large grid minors

Friday, 2025-08-01, 12:00

In the Vertex-Disjoint-Paths-With-Congestion problem, the input consists of a digraph D, an integer c and k pairs of vertices (s_i, t_i), and the task is to find a set of paths connecting each s_i to its corresponding t_i, whereas each vertex of D appears in at most c many paths. The case where c = 1 is known to be NP-complete even if k = 2 [Fortune, Hopcroft and Wyllie, 1980] on general digraphs and is W[1]-hard with respect to k (excluding the possibility of an f(k)n^O(1)-time algorithm under standard assumptions) on acyclic digraphs [Slivkins, 2010].
The proof of [Slivkins, 2010] can also be adapted to show W[1]-hardness with respect to k for every congestion c ≥ 1. We strengthen the existing hardness result by showing that the problem remains W[1]-hard for every congestion c ≥ 1 even if:

  1. the input digraph D is acyclic,
  2. D does not contain an acyclic (5, 5)-grid as a butterfly minor,
  3. D does not contain an acyclic tournament on 9 vertices as a butterfly minor, and
  4. D has ear-anonymity at most 5.
Further, we also show that the edge-congestion variant of the problem remains W[1]-hard for every congestion c ≥ 1 even if:
  1. the input digraph D is acyclic,
  2. D has maximum undirected degree 3,
  3. D does not contain an acyclic (7, 7)-wall as a weak immersion and
  4. D has ear-anonymity at most 5.
This is joint work with Ken-ichi Kawarabayashi, Nicola Lorenz and Jacob Stegemann.

Arnab Char (NII): On graphs with no induced P_5 or K_5-e

Friday, 2025-07-25, 12:00

A P_5 is an induced path on five vertices, and a K_5-e is a graph obtained from a complete graph on five vertices by removing an edge. A graph G is said to be (P_5, K_5− e)‐free if it does not have P_5 or K_5-e as an induced subgraph. In this talk, we present some results related to the chromatic number and the clique number for the class of (P_5, K_5− e)‐free graphs. Specifically, we show that if G is a connected (P_5, K_5− e)‐free graph with no clique cut-set and the clique number of at least 7, G is the complement of a bipartite graph, and there exists an imperfect connected graph G with the clique number of 6 and no clique cut-set. Additionally, we prove that every (P_5, K_5− e)‐free graph G with the clique number t > 3 is either 7-colorable or t-colorable. Our results, along with some known results, imply that the chromatic number problem can be solved in polynomial time for the class of (P_5, K_5 − e)‐free graphs.
This is a joint work with T. Karthick.

Themistoklis Haris: Compression Barriers in Autoregressive Transformers

Friday, 2025-07-18, 12:00

A key limitation of autoregressive Transformers is the large memory needed at inference-time to cache all previous key-value (KV) embeddings. Prior works address this by compressing the KV cache, but often assume specific structural properties of the embeddings. This raises the following natural question: Can truly sublinear space utilization be achieved without such assumptions? In this work, we answer this question in the negative. Any algorithm for attention-based token generation must use Θ(nd) space, where n is the number of tokens generated so far and d ≥ Ω(log n) is the dimension of the KV embeddings. Our proof involves a reduction from a classic communication complexity problem and uses a randomized construction that leverages properties of projections in the spirit of the Johnson-Linderstrauss lemma. For the low-dimensional regime d = o(log n), we show that any algorithm requires Ω(d e^d) space and prove, using tight bounds on covering numbers, that SubGen, proposed by Zandieh et al. [2024b], matches this bound. Further, we investigate how sparsity assumptions enable token generation in truly sublinear space, presenting impossibility results and proposing a new KV cache compression algorithm for sliding window attention when the value cache outside the window is unmasked. Finally, we analyze token generation’s time complexity, using an indistinguishability argument to prove that no non-adaptive algorithm can compute attention online in sublinear time for all tokens.

Cameron Seth: Testing Graph Properties with the Container Method

Friday, 2025-07-11, 12:00

How can we understand the structure of a massive graph by looking at only a tiny fraction of it? This is the central question behind property testing, a field that designs algorithms to quickly determine whether a graph has a global property, or is far from having it, using only a small random sample of its edges.
In this talk, I will introduce a powerful combinatorial technique called the container method, originally developed in extremal combinatorics, and show how it can be used to study fundamental questions in graph property testing. In particular, we will see how the container method can be used to analyze algorithms for the Independent Set Testing Problem, first introduced by Goldreich, Goldwasser and Ron (1998), which is the problem of distinguishing between graphs that have a large independent set and graphs that are far from having such an independent set.
This is joint work with Eric Blais.

Pengxiang Wang: Time-Space Tradeoffs of Truncation with Preprocessing

Friday, 2025-07-04, 12:00

Truncation of cryptographic outputs is a technique that was recently introduced in Baldimtsi et al. [BCCK22]. The general idea is to try out many inputs to some cryptographic algorithm until the output (e.g. a public-key or some hash value) falls into some sparse set and thus can be compressed: by trying out an expected $2^k$ different inputs one will find an output that starts with $k$ zeros.
Using such truncation one can for example save substantial gas fees on Blockchains where storing values is very expensive. While [BCCK22] show that truncation preserves the security of the underlying primitive, they only consider a setting without preprocessing. In this work we show that lower bounds on the time-space tradeoff for inverting random functions and permutations also hold with truncation, except for parameters ranges where the bound fails to hold for ``trivial'' reasons. Concretely, it's known that any algorithm that inverts a random function or permutation with range $N$ making $T$ queries and using $S$ bits of auxiliary input must satisfy $S\cdot T\ge N\log N$.
This lower bound no longer holds in the truncated setting where one must only invert a challenge from a range of size $N/2^k$, as now one can simply save the replies to all $N/2^k$ challenges, which requires $S=\log N\cdot N /2^k$ bits and allows to invert with $T=1$ query.
We show that with truncation, whenever $S$ is somewhat smaller than the $\log N\cdot N /2^k$ bits required to store the entire truncated function table, the known $S\cdot T\ge N\log N$ lower bound applies.

Gaia Carenini: Modular chromatic index of graphs

Friday, 2025-06-27, 12:00

Let X''_k(G) denote the minimum number of colors needed to color the edges of a graph G in such a way that the subgraph spanned by the edges of any single color has all positive degrees congruent to 1 modulo k. Pyber (1992) proved that X''_2(G) < 4 for every graph G, and asked whether X''_k(G) can be bounded by a function of k alone for every k ≥ 3. Scott (1997) answered Pyber's question by proving that X''_k(G) ≤ 5k² log k, and further asked whether X''_k(G) = O(k). In 2022, Botler, Colucci, and Kohayakawa (2023) answered Scott’s question affirmatively by proving that X''_k(G) ≤ 198k − 101, and conjectured that the multiplicative constant could be reduced to 1.In 2024, Nweit and Yang made progress toward this conjecture, proving that X''_k(G) ≤ 177k − 93. In this work, we improve this constant to 9. More specifically, we prove that X''_k(G) ≤ 7k + o(k) if k is odd, and X''_k(G) ≤ 9k + o(k) if k is even.
This is a joint work with Gaetan Berthe, Marthe Bonamy, Fabio Botler, Arthur Dumas, and Fatemeh Ghasemi.

Meike Hatzel: The Erdős-Pósa property for vertex-minors

Friday, 2025-06-20, 12:00

The talk presents the following Erdős-Pósa result for vertex-minors: For every circle graph $H$ and integer $k$, there exists an integer $c = c(k, H)$ such that every graph $G$ either

  • has a vertex-minor isomorphic to the disjoint union of k copies of $H$, or
  • there is a $c$-perturbation $\tilde{G}$ of $G$ and a set $X \subseteq V(G)$ of size at most $c$ such that $\tilde{G}−X$ has no vertex-minor isomorphic to $H$.
This is joint work with Rutger Campbell, J. Pascal Gollin, O-joung Kwon, Rose McCarty, Sang-il Oum, and Sebastian Wiederrecht.

Quanquan Liu: The Predicted-Updates Dynamic Model

Friday, 2025-06-13, 11:30

The main bottleneck in designing efficient dynamic algorithms is the unknown nature of the update sequence. In particular, there are some problems, like triconnectivity, planar digraph all pairs shortest paths, k-edge connectivity, and others, where the separation in runtime between the best offline or partially dynamic solutions and the best fully dynamic solutions is polynomial, sometimes even exponential.
In this talk, we introduce the predicted-updates dynamic model, one of the first beyond-worst-case models for dynamic algorithms, which generalizes a large set of well-studied dynamic models including the offline dynamic, incremental, and decremental models to the fully dynamic setting when given predictions about the update times of the elements. In the most basic form of our model, we receive a set of predicted update times for all of the updates that occur over the event horizon. We give a novel framework that “lifts” offline divide-and-conquer algorithms into the fully dynamic setting with little overhead. Using this, we are able to interpolate between the offline and fully dynamic settings; when the $\ell_1$ error of the prediction is linear in the number of updates, we achieve the offline runtime of the algorithm (up to poly log n factors).
Joint work with Vaidehi Srinivas, appeared in COLT 2024.

Amadeus Reinald: Plane strong connectivity augmentation

Friday, 2025-06-06, 12:00

Given a network of non-crossing roads between cities, where not all of them are reachable from one-another, how to build a minimal number of roads such making all cities interconnected? Formally, we consider the problem asking to augment a plane oriented graph by adding a minimal number of arcs, to obtain a strongly connected oriented graph which is still planar. In this talk, we show that, unlike unrestricted versions of the problem, Plane Strong Connectivity Augmentation is NP-complete. On the positive side, we obtain an efficient FPT (parameterized) algorithm, running in polynomial-time when the number of arc additions is bounded.
This is joint work with Stéphane Bessy, Daniel Gonçalves and Dimitrios Thilikos.

Kazuki Watanabe (NII): Multi-objective Optimization Meets Model Checking

Friday, 2025-05-30, 12:00

Model checking provides a rigorous framework for certifying software correctness by generating formal proofs or counterexamples. In this talk, we present recent progress in compositional model checking, a methodology that leverages the modular structure of software to improve scalability and tractability. Intriguingly, we uncover a deep and fruitful connection between compositional model checking and multi-objective optimization. This connection leads to a novel, quantitative caching technique that enhances the efficiency of compositional reasoning. We illustrate the effectiveness of this quantitative cache through its application to compositional probabilistic model checking of Markov decision processes. Finally, we outline several promising directions for future research at the intersection of formal verification and continuous optimization.

Orane Belhomme: An extension of CTL for synchronization problems

Friday, 2025-05-23, 12:00

In the community of model checking, Computation Tree Logic (CTL) is a very useful tool used to describe the properties of a system over time. It allows researchers to construct models in which they can effectively study behaviours and patterns.
Around 2020, my internship supervisor (Nicolas Markey) began to think about an extension of CTL that would allow for an easier description of synchronization phenomena. In model checking, this could be useful when one wants to speak about a system in which a property will have the same truth value after a given number of steps, no matter what the execution is. The basis of my internship was precisely to design these new operators for CTL, and then to study their characteristics. This is what I will talk about after a quick introduction to temporal logics in model checking.

Max Dupre la Tour: Characterizing and Recognizing Leaf Powers

Friday, 2025-05-16, 12:00

Leaf powers were introduced 20 years ago by Nishimura, Ragde, and Thilikos as a simplified model for phylogenetic tree reconstruction.
A graph G is a *k*-leaf power if there exists a tree T whose leaves are the vertices of G, with the property that a pair of leaves u and v induce an edge in G if and only if they are at most distance *k* apart in T.
I will present some results on how to characterize and recognize such graphs.

Samuel Coulomb: Directed Clique Number and the Gyárfás-Sumner Conjecture for Tournaments.

Friday, 2025-05-09, 12:00

The chromatic number and the clique number are fundamental parameters in graph theory, with the clique number serving as a natural lower bound for the chromatic number. However, graphs with bounded clique number can have arbitrarily large chromatic number. The study of when the chromatic number is bounded by a function of the clique number is known as chi-boundedness. A central question in this area is the Gyárfás-Sumner conjecture: which induced subgraph must appear in any graph with bounded clique number and sufficiently large chromatic number?
In the directed graph setting, the dichromatic number is widely accepted as the generalization of the chromatic number, but defining an analog to the clique number is non-trivial. A novel definition has recently been introduced and, while less intuitive, exhibits better properties compared to previous attempts. We discuss its interest and explore the Gyárfás-Sumner conjecture within this new framework.

Zhiye Xie: No Time to Hash: On Super Efficient Entropy Accumulation

Friday, 2025-04-18, 12:00

Real-world random number generators cannot afford to use cryptographic hashing every time they refresh their state with a new entropic input. Instead, they use “superefficient” simple entropy-accumulation procedures like bitwise rotation and XOR with new entropy. For instance, Microsoft’s RNG uses bitwise rotations with amount 5 (for 32-bit CPU) and 19 (for 64-bit CPU). But where do these rotation amounts come from? Are they optimal? Should we replace rotation with a better permutation?
In this take, we present a formal study of these questions by modeling entropy inputs as independent samples from a natural family of distributions, which we call 2-monotone distributions (including Gaussian, exponential, etc.). Our main contributions:

  • We prove that for any bitwise rotation with coprime amount α, entropy accumulates at an optimal Ω(n) rate over n steps.
  • However, some rotation values are better than others. For instance, α=19 is near-optimal for 64-bit CPU, while α=7 generally outperforms α=5 for 32-bit CPU.
  • More generally, we analyze arbitrary bit permutations π, and introduce a parameter—called covering number —which characterizes how quickly entropy accumulates for inputs with min-entropy k.
  • Finally, we construct a simple permutation that achieves near-optimal performance across all k, and we validate its advantage over standard rotations in experiments.
The talk is based on the paper appeared in CRYPTO 2021.
