Chunyang Wang: Local Gibbs Sampling beyond Local Uniformity

Friday, 2025-11-21, 12:00

Local samplers are algorithms that generate random samples based on local queries to high-dimensional distributions, ensuring the samples follow the correct induced distributions while maintaining time complexity that scales locally with the query size. These samplers have broad applications, including deterministic approximate counting~[He, Wang, Yin, SODA '23; Feng et.al., FOCS '23], sampling from infinite or high-dimensional Gibbs distributions~[Anand, Jerrum, SICOMP '22; He, Wang, Yin, FOCS '22], and providing local access to large random objects~[Biswas, Rubinfield, Yodpinyanee, ITCS '20].In this work, we present local samplers for Gibbs distributions of spin systems. Specifically, we design linear-time local samplers for spin systems with soft constraints, including the first local sampler for near-critical Ising models, as well as for truly repulsive spin systems, represented by the first local sampler for uniform proper $q$-colorings, with $q=O(\Delta)$ colors on graphs with maximum degree $\Delta$.
These local samplers are efficient beyond the ``local uniformity'' threshold, which imposes unconditional marginal lower bounds --- a key assumption required by all prior local samplers. Our results show that, in general, local sampling is not significantly harder than global sampling for spin systems. As an application, our results also imply local algorithms for probabilistic inference in the same near-critical regimes.

Halley Goldberg: Probabilistic Kolmogorov Complexity with Applications to Average-Case Complexity

Friday, 2025-11-14, 12:00

It is a central open problem in complexity theory to understand the relationship between worst-case and average-case complexity, particularly of the class NP. Hirahara (STOC 2021) made exciting progress toward this goal by applying techniques from meta-complexity. He showed, for example, that if DistNP^NP ⊆ AvgP then NP ⊆ TIME[2^{O(n/log n)}]. However, the original proof does not survive the setting of probabilistic computation.
In this talk, I will discuss the introduction of a probabilistic variant of time-bounded Kolmogorov complexity, called pK^t complexity, which allows the above limitation to be overcome. In the few years since the aforementioned work, pK^t complexity has also found broader utility for meta-complexity and related subjects.
Starting from basic definitions and background, the talk will lead to a proof overview of the following statement: if DistNP^NP ⊆ AvgBPP then NP ⊆ BPTIME[2^{O(n/log n)}].
Based on joint work with Valentine Kabanets, Zhenjian Lu, and Igor Oliveira.

Petr Chmel: String graphs with precise number of intersections

Friday, 2025-11-07, 12:00

A string graph is an intersection graph of curves in the plane. A k-string graph is a graph with a string representation in which every pair of curves intersects in at most k points. In this talk, we introduce the class of (=k)-string graphs as a further restriction of k-string graphs by requiring that every two curves intersect in either zero or precisely k points. We study the hierarchy of these graphs, showing that for any k ≥ 1, (=k)-string graphs are a subclass of (=k+2)-string graphs as well as of (=4k)-string graphs; however, there are no other inclusions between the classes of (=k)-string and (=ℓ)-string graphs apart from those that are implied by the above rules. In particular, the classes of (=k)-string graphs and (=k+1)-string graphs are incomparable by inclusion for any k, and the class of (=2)-string graphs is not contained in the class of (=2ℓ+1)-string graphs for any ℓ.
Joint work with Vít Jelínek.

Lucas Picasarri-Arrieta: Chromatic discrepancy of locally s-colourable graphs

Friday, 2025-10-31

The chromatic discrepancy of a graph G, denoted φ(G), is the least over all proper colourings σ of G of the greatest difference between the number of colours |σ(V (H))| spanned by an induced subgraph H of G and its chromatic number χ(H). It follows easily from the definition that every graph G satisfies φ(G) ∈ [0, χ(G)-1]. In this talk, we will see that locally colourable graphs actually have chromatic discrepancy close to the trivial upper bound χ(G)-1. In particular, we will show that triangle free graphs have chromatic discrepancy either χ(G)-2 or χ(G)-1, hence confirming the remaining open case of a conjecture of Aravind, Kalyanasundaram, Sandeep, and Sivadasan. Along the way, we will prove an (almost) tight bound on the chromatic number of balls of radius ℓ/2 in graphs excluding the cycle on ℓ+1, which could be of independent interest.
Joint work with Timothée Corsini, Théo Pierron, François Pirot, and Eileen Robinson.
Preprint available at https://arxiv.org/pdf/2508.02985.
